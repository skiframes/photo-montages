Hey racer! This website has cool photos and videos from your race. Here's how to find yours:

Finding Your Stuff

  1. Click "Results" at the top of the page
  2. Pick your age group (like U12 Girls or U14 Boys)
  3. Find your name in the list, or type it in the search box
  4. Click the green buttons next to your name:
    • PM = Photo Montage (a cool picture showing your whole run!)
    • V = Video clip of you skiing

What's a Photo Montage?

It's like a magic picture that shows YOU at every gate — all in one image! You can see your whole run from start to finish. It's great for seeing your body position and how you moved through the gates.

Why Don't I Have All Sections?

Sometimes a section might be missing for you. Don't worry — it's not your fault! Here's why it happens:

  • A coach or course worker was near the camera helping another racer
  • Someone was fixing a gate while you skied by
  • The camera saw too many people moving at once and got confused

The cameras are pretty smart, but sometimes they can't tell you apart from the helpers on the course!

Download Your Photos

Want to save your photo or video? Click the Download button when viewing it. You can share it with your friends and family!

What About the Times?

The "Time*" numbers next to each section are estimates — they're close but not official like the times from the timing wands. Use them to compare with your teammates, but remember the finish time is what really counts!

Overview for Adults

This section provides a comprehensive overview of Skiframes for coaches, parents, and other adults interested in understanding how the system works.

What is Skiframes?

Skiframes is an open-source video analytics platform for alpine ski racing. It automatically generates stop-motion photo montages from race video, showing an athlete's full run through a course section in a single image.

The technique is inspired by Ron LeMaster's ski photography from Ultimate Skiing, where multiple exposures of a skier are composited onto a single background to reveal body position, line, and timing through a gate sequence.

How It Works

Cameras positioned along the race course record video at 30 frames per second. Software detects each skier entering and leaving the camera's field of view, extracts the relevant frames, and composites them into a single montage image. The result shows the skier's progression through 4-8 gates in one picture.

  [30fps video] ---> [Frame differencing] ---> [Stop-motion composite]
       |                         |                              |
  Records all day     Triggers on motion         One image per athlete

Understanding Results

Official vs. Unofficial Times

The Rank and Time columns show official results from the Vola electronic timing system (wired timing wands at start and finish). These are the authoritative race results.

The Section times (under each camera section) are unofficial estimates derived from video analysis. They show how long the athlete was visible in each camera's field of view. Accuracy is approximately ±0.08 seconds.

Section times are useful as a coaching tool to compare athletes' speed through different parts of the course, but they should not be used for any official purpose.

FPS Variants Explained

Each photo montage is generated at multiple frame rates:

Missing Sections

Some athletes may have missing sections (no photo montage or video available). This typically happens when course crew were working nearby — maintaining the course, resetting gates, or assisting racers — which created overlapping motion in the camera's trigger zones.

When multiple people are moving in the camera's field of view simultaneously, it becomes impossible to cleanly isolate the skier's run for the composite image. This is a limitation of automated detection and not a reflection of the athlete's performance.

Camera Setup

Hardware

The system uses three Reolink RLC-823S2 cameras (2K resolution, 30fps, 5Mbps RTSP streams). Each camera covers a different section of the race course.

Cameras connect to the edge compute device via ethernet or WiFi. The edge device connects to the internet via a 5G uplink (Teltonika RUTM50 router) for remote access and S3 uploads.

Camera Placement

Position cameras to capture 2-8 gates each. Key considerations:

  • Field of view — ~55° horizontal. Position far enough to capture the full gate sequence.
  • Angle — Looking uphill or across the slope. Avoid looking directly downhill (skiers approach head-on with little visible body movement).
  • Stability — Use heavy-duty tripods. Wind and vibration cause misaligned composites.
  • Overlap — Slight gate overlap between cameras is fine; gaps mean missed coverage.

Trigger Zone Concept

The gates being filmed are between the START and END zones. When a skier enters the START zone, the system begins tracking. When they exit the END zone, the run is complete and a montage is generated.

The calibration system can automatically detect ski gates in the camera frame using HSV color filtering:

hsv = cv2.cvtColor(frame, COLOR_BGR2HSV)

# Red gates (hue wraps around 0/180 in HSV)
red_mask = inRange(hsv, (0, 120, 80), (10, 255, 255)) |
           inRange(hsv, (170, 120, 80), (180, 255, 255))

# Blue gates
blue_mask = inRange(hsv, (100, 120, 80), (130, 255, 255))

# Aspect ratio filter: gates are tall and thin
for contour in findContours(mask):
    x, y, w, h = boundingRect(contour)
    if h > 3 * w and h > 30:
        gates.append({"color": color, "top": (x+w//2, y), "base": (x+w//2, y+h)})

Tuning Detection

Detection sensitivity can be adjusted in the calibration UI. Changes take effect immediately (hot-reload every 2 seconds) without restarting detection.

Parameters

ParameterDefaultRangeEffect
detection_threshold 25 0-255 Minimum pixel difference to count as changed. Higher = less sensitive. Increase if shadows or snow spray cause false triggers.
min_pixel_change_pct 5.0% 0-100% Minimum percentage of zone pixels that must change. Higher = requires more movement. Increase for noisy/windy conditions.
min_brightness 100 0-255 Changed pixels must be at least this bright. Filters shadows (dark) while keeping skiers (bright against snow). Key for sunny days.
cooldown_seconds 3 0-30 Minimum time between consecutive runs. Prevents double-triggers from same skier.

Troubleshooting

  • Too many false positives — Increase detection_threshold to 35-45, increase min_brightness to 120-150
  • Missing runs — Decrease detection_threshold to 15-20, decrease min_pixel_change_pct to 3%
  • Shadow triggers — Increase min_brightness to 130+ (shadows are darker than skiers)
  • Wind/snow triggers — Increase min_pixel_change_pct to 8-10%
  • Double-counting same skier — Increase cooldown_seconds to 5-8
How detection parameters interact

For each video frame, the detection engine:

  1. Converts frame to grayscale
  2. Computes absolute difference with previous frame: diff = |frame_n - frame_n-1|
  3. Crops to the trigger zone region
  4. Thresholds: pixels where diff > detection_threshold are "changed"
  5. Brightness filter: only keeps changed pixels where brightness > min_brightness
  6. Counts remaining changed pixels as percentage of zone area
  7. If percentage > min_pixel_change_pct, the zone is triggered

The brightness filter is the key innovation for ski racing: snow is bright (200+), skiers are moderately bright (120-200), but shadows are dark (30-80). By requiring changed pixels to be bright, shadows are filtered out while skiers pass through.

Detection Engine

The detection engine (detection.py) monitors video frames for skier motion using frame differencing — a classical computer vision technique, not machine learning. It compares consecutive frames pixel-by-pixel to detect movement within configurable trigger zones.

Core Algorithm

For every frame received from the camera (at ~10fps during idle, full 30fps during active tracking):

  1. Extract zone ROI — Crop both current and previous frames to the trigger zone rectangle
  2. Absolute difference — diff = cv2.absdiff(prev_zone, curr_zone) produces a grayscale difference image
  3. Threshold — Pixels where diff > detection_threshold (default 25, range 0–255) are marked as "changed"
  4. Brightness filter — Of the changed pixels, only keep those where current_brightness ≥ min_brightness (default 100). This is the key innovation for ski racing: snow is bright (200+), skiers are moderately bright (120–200), but shadows are dark (30–80)
  5. Percentage calculation — pct = changed_pixels / total_zone_pixels × 100
  6. Trigger decision — If pct > min_pixel_change_pct (default 5%), the zone fires
# Actual motion detection logic (simplified from detection.py)
def detect_motion_in_zone(prev_frame, curr_frame, zone, config):
    prev_roi = prev_frame[zone.y1:zone.y2, zone.x1:zone.x2]
    curr_roi = curr_frame[zone.y1:zone.y2, zone.x1:zone.x2]

    diff = cv2.absdiff(prev_roi, curr_roi)
    gray_diff = cv2.cvtColor(diff, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)

    # Step 1: Which pixels changed enough?
    motion_mask = gray_diff > config.detection_threshold

    # Step 2: Of those, which are bright enough? (filters shadows)
    if config.min_brightness > 0:
        brightness = cv2.cvtColor(curr_roi, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)
        motion_mask = motion_mask & (brightness >= config.min_brightness)

    # Step 3: What percentage of zone area changed?
    pct = np.sum(motion_mask) / motion_mask.size * 100
    return pct > config.min_pixel_change_pct, pct

Why Frame Differencing Instead of AI/ML?

Frame differencing was chosen deliberately over neural-network-based object detection (YOLO, etc.) for several reasons:

  • Deterministic — No model weights, no inference uncertainty, no GPU required. Results are reproducible and explainable.
  • Fast — A single absdiff + threshold + mask takes <1ms per frame. YOLO inference takes 15–50ms. On a 3-camera system, this saves ~150ms per frame cycle.
  • Snow-optimized — The brightness filter exploits the unique property of ski racing: bright snow background with darker skier silhouettes. ML detectors trained on general scenes struggle with white-on-white conditions.
  • No training data needed — Works immediately on any camera angle, any course. ML models require labeled training data for each new environment.
  • Tunable in real-time — Coaches adjust sensitivity from their phone. Detection parameters hot-reload every 2 seconds without restarting the engine.

The trade-off is that frame differencing cannot distinguish what is moving — only that something moved. Course workers, falling gates, and blowing snow can all trigger detections. This is mitigated by the brightness filter and by careful trigger zone placement.

State Machine

The detection engine operates as a three-state finite automaton:

  IDLE  ──(START zone triggered)──▶  TRACKING
    ▲                                     │
    │                                     │
    └──(cooldown expires)── COMPLETE ◀────┤── (END zone triggered)
                                │         └── (timeout 30s)
                                │         └── (duration elapsed)
                                ▼
                          on_run_complete callback
                          ├── Generate montage(s)
                          ├── Generate video clip
                          ├── Match to Vola athlete
                          └── Write timing JSON

State Transitions

FromToCondition
IDLETRACKINGSTART zone fires AND cooldown elapsed since last trigger
TRACKINGCOMPLETEEND zone fires (zone mode) OR duration elapsed (duration mode) OR 30s timeout
COMPLETEIDLEAfter cooldown_seconds (default 3s) — prevents re-triggering on same skier

Two Completion Modes

  • Zone mode (default) — Run ends when the END zone detects motion. Best for races with clearly separated skier paths.
  • Duration mode — Run ends after a fixed number of seconds (run_duration_seconds). Used when the END zone can’t be reliably placed, or when Vola timing data provides known section durations per athlete.

Detection Sampling Rate

The engine runs at two speeds to balance CPU usage and responsiveness:

StateFrame Check RateFrame Capture RateWhy
IDLE~10fps (every 3rd frame)None (but FrameBuffer stores all)Save CPU; 10fps is fast enough to catch a skier entering
TRACKING30fps (every frame)30fps (all frames to run.frames)Full resolution for montage quality

The detection interval is computed as max(1, int(fps / 10)) — at 30fps, that’s every 3rd frame. During active tracking, every frame is both checked (for END zone) and captured (for montage generation).

FrameBuffer and pre-trigger capture

The FrameBuffer continuously stores the most recent frames (default: 2 seconds worth at 30fps = 60 frames). When the START zone triggers, the buffered frames are included in the run, capturing the skier's approach before the trigger fired. This ensures no part of the run is missed due to detection latency.

# Ring buffer implementation
buffer = deque(maxlen=int(fps * pre_buffer_seconds) + 1)

def process_frame(frame, frame_num, timestamp):
    buffer.append(frame.copy())  # Copy! Original may be overwritten

    if state == IDLE:
        if frame_num % detection_interval == 0:  # ~10fps check
            triggered, pct = detect_motion(start_zone)
            if triggered and cooldown_elapsed:
                run = Run(frames=list(buffer))  # Pre-trigger frames included!
                state = TRACKING

    elif state == TRACKING:
        run.frames.append(frame.copy())  # Every frame at 30fps
        triggered, pct = detect_motion(end_zone)
        if triggered or elapsed > 30:
            state = COMPLETE
            on_run_complete(run)
Stream Manager for shared RTSP connections

When multiple detection sessions run on the same camera (e.g., different age groups or coaches monitoring different zones), the Stream Manager (stream_manager.py) maintains a single RTSP connection per camera and distributes frames to all registered sessions. This avoids duplicate decode overhead and connection limits.

Architecture: StreamManager → CameraStream (one per camera) → multiple DetectionEngine sessions.

# Frame distribution in CameraStream thread
for session_id, session in self.sessions.items():
    engine = session['engine']
    if engine.current_run is not None:
        engine.process_frame(frame, frame_num, now)  # Every frame during run
    elif frame_num % check_interval == 0:
        engine.process_frame(frame, frame_num, now)  # ~10fps idle

Montage generation uses a ThreadPoolExecutor for non-blocking async processing. When a run completes, the callback is submitted to the thread pool so the detection engine can immediately resume monitoring for the next skier.

RTSP options: rtsp_transport=tcp|fflags=+genpts (TCP for reliability; genpts for Reolink timestamp quirks). Up to 10 reconnect attempts before giving up on a camera.

Montage Generation

The montage generator (montage.py) creates stop-motion photo composites from the frames captured during a run. The technique is inspired by Ron LeMaster's ski photography from Ultimate Skiing.

Pixel-Wise Minimum Algorithm

The core technique is remarkably simple: for each pixel position, take the darkest value across all frames. Since the snow background is bright (white) and the skier is darker, the skier's silhouette from every frame is preserved while the snow background remains clean.

  Frame 1        Frame 2        Frame 3        Composite
  ┌────────┐    ┌────────┐    ┌────────┐    ┌────────────┐
  │  ○     │    │    ○   │    │      ○ │    │  ○  ○   ○  │
  │  |     │    │    |   │    │      | │    │  |  |   |  │
  │ / \    │    │   / \  │    │    / \ │ => │ / \/ \ / \ │
  │ snow   │    │ snow   │    │ snow   │    │ snow        │
  └────────┘    └────────┘    └────────┘    └────────────┘

  np.minimum(frame1, frame2, frame3, ...)
  Keeps darkest pixel at each position

In NumPy, the entire composite is a single line: result = np.minimum(result, next_frame) applied iteratively. Processing ~25 frames at 4K resolution takes approximately 0.4 seconds.

Processing Pipeline

  1. Frame selection — Subsample captured frames to target FPS (e.g., 30fps capture → 6fps montage = every 5th frame)
  2. Crop — Compute bounding box from START/END zones with asymmetric padding:
    • Left padding: 30% of zone width (captures approach line)
    • Right padding: 50% of zone width (captures exit line — skiers drift further right)
    • Vertical padding: 10% (slight buffer above/below)
    • All values clamped to frame bounds (0–3840 horizontal, 0–2160 vertical)
  3. Bilateral filter — Edge-preserving noise reduction applied to each frame before compositing: cv2.bilateralFilter(f, d=5, sigmaColor=50, sigmaSpace=50)
  4. Composite — Iterative pixel-wise minimum: result = np.minimum(result, next_frame)
  5. Overlay — Add race metadata (event, discipline, age, run, date), elapsed time, gate measurements, and sponsor logos
  6. Output — Generate thumbnail (~800px width, JPEG Q85, ~200KB) and full-resolution (native crop, JPEG Q95)
# Complete montage generation (simplified from montage.py)
def create_montage(frames, crop_region):
    # 1. Crop all frames to the region of interest
    cropped = [f[crop.y:crop.y+crop.h, crop.x:crop.x+crop.w] for f in frames]

    # 2. Bilateral filter: reduce noise, preserve edges
    filtered = [cv2.bilateralFilter(f, 5, 50, 50) for f in cropped]

    # 3. Pixel-wise minimum (darkest pixel wins)
    composite = filtered[0].copy()
    for frame in filtered[1:]:
        np.minimum(composite, frame, out=composite)

    # 4. Generate outputs
    cv2.imwrite(fullres_path, composite, [IMWRITE_JPEG_QUALITY, 95])
    thumb = cv2.resize(composite, (800, auto_h), INTER_AREA)
    cv2.imwrite(thumb_path, thumb, [IMWRITE_JPEG_QUALITY, 85])

Why bilateral filter instead of Gaussian blur?

Gaussian blur smooths everything equally, including edges. Bilateral filtering smooths flat areas (reducing sensor noise in the snow) while preserving sharp edges (keeping the skier's outline crisp). Quality comparison from production montages:

MetricRaw MinimumWith Bilateral FilterImprovement
Noise level (std dev)221341% reduction
Edge sharpness (Laplacian)301728735% reduction (acceptable)
Processing time (25 frames)—+0.15s overhead—

The trade-off is worthwhile: nearly half the noise removed with minimal sharpness loss. The bilateral filter parameters (d=5, sigmaColor=50, sigmaSpace=50) were tuned empirically on 200+ race montages.

FPS variant generation

Each run generates montages at multiple frame rates (typically 5.0, 6.0, 7.0 images per second for races; 4.0, 2.0 for training). This is done by subsampling the captured frames differently:

# 30fps video, 2.5-second run = 75 frames captured
# At 5.0 img/s: interval = 30/5 = 6.0 → every 6th frame = 12 in composite
# At 6.0 img/s: interval = 30/6 = 5.0 → every 5th frame = 15 in composite
# At 7.0 img/s: interval = 30/7 ≈ 4.3 → every ~4th frame = 17 in composite

def select_frames_by_fps(frames, source_fps, target_fps):
    interval = source_fps / target_fps
    selected = []
    next_frame = 0.0
    for i, frame in enumerate(frames):
        if i >= next_frame:
            selected.append(frame)
            next_frame += interval
    return selected

More frames = smoother motion trail but more overlap between positions. Fewer frames = cleaner individual positions but less continuous flow. The default (6.0 img/s) is a good middle ground for races. Training sessions use lower rates (4.0) since runs are longer.

Video clip generation

In addition to the static montage image, a video clip is generated from the captured frames:

  1. Frames are cropped to the same region as the montage
  2. Encoded to H.264 MP4 at the original source framerate (30fps)
  3. Race metadata and logos are overlaid on each frame
  4. Output provides normal-speed and slow-motion playback capability in the gallery

Video clips are typically 2–5 seconds long and 2–8MB in size depending on the section length and resolution.

Athlete Re-Identification (AI Model)

During training sessions (not races), the system uses a pre-trained CLIP vision model to generate visual embeddings for each detected skier. These embeddings enable automatic grouping of montages by athlete — even without bib numbers or timing data.

Why AI for Training but Not Races?

ContextIdentification MethodReason
RacesVola timing data (bib + timestamp)Official timing provides deterministic athlete-to-run matching. Athletes run in known order with recorded start times.
TrainingCLIP embeddings + clusteringNo bib numbers, no timing system, athletes run in random order. Visual appearance is the only signal available.

The CLIP Embedding Model

CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training) is a vision model developed by OpenAI that learns visual concepts from natural language supervision. Skiframes uses only the vision encoder to generate a 512-dimensional embedding vector for each detected skier.

How the Model Was Trained

The CLIP model was not trained by Skiframes — it is a pre-trained foundation model. Key training details:

  • Training data — 400 million image-text pairs scraped from the internet (WebImageText dataset)
  • Architecture — Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32): 12 transformer layers, 768-dim hidden state, 32×32 patch size
  • Objective — Contrastive learning: match images to their text descriptions. This produces general-purpose visual features that transfer well to downstream tasks
  • Output — 512-dimensional L2-normalized embedding vector per image
  • Published — OpenAI, January 2021 (“Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision”)

The key insight is that CLIP's general visual features are rich enough to distinguish individual skiers by their helmet color, jacket pattern, ski poles, and body proportions — without any ski-racing-specific fine-tuning.

Embedding Generation Pipeline

  1. Frame selection — From the captured run frames, select representative frames (middle of run, when skier is most visible)
  2. Crop to skier region — Use the trigger zone crop region to focus on the area containing the skier
  3. CLIP inference — Pass cropped frames through ViT-B/32 vision encoder
  4. Average pooling — Average embeddings across selected frames to get a single 512-dim vector per run
  5. Store in manifest — The embedding vector is saved alongside the montage in the session manifest JSON

Athlete Clustering

In the web gallery, embeddings are used for unsupervised clustering to group montages by athlete:

  1. Pairwise cosine similarity — Compute similarity between all pairs of embeddings. Same athlete: typically >0.85. Different athletes: typically <0.70.
  2. Agglomerative clustering — Group runs where cosine similarity exceeds a configurable threshold (default ~0.78)
  3. Gallery display — The “Athlete View” toggle groups montages by cluster, showing all runs by the same person together
  4. Manual correction — Coaches can drag-and-drop montages between clusters to fix grouping errors

Limitations

  • Clothing changes — If an athlete changes jacket or helmet between sessions, the embeddings won't match
  • Identical gear — Teammates wearing the same team jacket may be confused. Body proportions help but aren't always sufficient.
  • Overcast/flat light — Low-contrast conditions reduce the distinctiveness of color features
  • Not used for races — Race identification relies entirely on Vola timing data, which is deterministic

Vola Timing Integration

For race events, athlete identification uses the Vola electronic timing system rather than visual recognition. This provides deterministic, reliable matching between detected runs and athletes.

Data Flow

  1. Vola CSV export — The timing operator exports a CSV file from the Vola timing software containing athlete names, bib numbers, clubs, and start-of-day times
  2. Camera offset calculation — For each athlete, the system estimates when they'll appear in each camera's field of view by adding a fixed offset to their start time (based on how long it takes to ski from the start gate to that camera section)
  3. Timestamp matching — When the detection engine completes a run, the run's timestamp is compared against the Vola-predicted camera times with a ±10 second tolerance window
  4. Bib assignment — The matched athlete's bib number determines the output filename and manifest entry
# Athlete matching logic (from runner.py)
def find_matching_racer(run_start_time):
    run_sec = run_start_time.hour * 3600 + minute * 60 + second
    tolerance = 10.0  # seconds

    for racer in vola_racers:
        if racer already matched: continue
        cam_start = racer['camera_start_sec']
        cam_end   = racer['camera_end_sec']
        if (cam_start - tolerance) <= run_sec <= (cam_end + tolerance):
            return racer  # Match found!
    return None  # Unmatched run → named by timestamp

Early Stopping Optimization

When processing recorded video files in batch mode, the runner implements two early-stopping heuristics to avoid processing unnecessary video:

  • All athletes matched — If every racer in the Vola list has been paired with a detected run, processing stops immediately
  • Past timing window — If the video timestamp exceeds the last racer's predicted end time by 120 seconds and no run is currently active, processing stops

3D Terrain Pipeline

The 3D course visualization renders the actual race terrain using a digital elevation model and satellite imagery in Three.js.

Data Sources

DataSourceResolutionFile
ElevationUSGS 1m DEM (LiDAR)1 meter/pixelterrain_heightmap.bin
Satellite imageryEsri World Imagery~0.3m/pixel (zoom 19)terrain_texture.png
Terrain metadataExtracted from DEM—terrain_meta.json

Rendering Pipeline

  1. Load heightmap — Binary file of 16-bit elevation values, 800×800 pixels
  2. Create geometry — Three.js PlaneGeometry with vertices displaced by elevation values
  3. Apply texture — Satellite or hillshade image mapped onto the terrain surface
  4. Add course overlay — Gate spheres at GPS positions, projected onto terrain surface
  5. Add section shading — Colored polygon meshes for each camera's coverage area
  6. Lighting — Ambient (0.55) + directional sun (0.85) + fill light (0.3)
GPS to 3D coordinate mapping

GPS coordinates (latitude, longitude, elevation) are converted to Three.js world coordinates using the terrain metadata:

function gpsTo3D(lat, lon, elev) {
  // Normalize to terrain grid position (0..width, 0..height)
  const x = (lon - bounds.west) / (bounds.east - bounds.west) * terrainWidth;
  const z = (lat - bounds.north) / (bounds.south - bounds.north) * terrainHeight;

  // Elevation scaled by terrain metadata
  const y = (elev - elevMin) * elevScale;

  return { x, y, z };
}

The terrain covers approximately 800×800 meters centered on the race course, with elevation ranging from ~184m to ~330m above sea level.

Terrain extraction process

The extract_terrain.py script processes a USGS GeoTIFF into web-ready formats:

  1. Download 1m DEM tile from USGS National Map
  2. Crop to area of interest (race course + buffer)
  3. Sample elevation at 2m intervals (reducing 800m extent to 400 samples)
  4. Quantize to 16-bit unsigned integers
  5. Write binary heightmap + metadata JSON
  6. Generate hillshade texture using sun angle computation
  7. Fetch satellite tiles from Esri and composite into a single texture
